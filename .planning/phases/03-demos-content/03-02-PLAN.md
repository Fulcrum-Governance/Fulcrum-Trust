**Section 1 — Opening blockquote:**
Lead with this exact blockquote: "In November 2025, a team using LangChain agents racked up $47,000 in API costs over 11 days before anyone noticed. **Section 2 — ## What Actually Happened (~200 words):**
Describe the gratitude loop pattern in plain terms. No jargon. Two agents responding to each other's outputs indefinitely. Each output is valid (non-empty, non-error). Neither agent ever produces "task complete." The system has no way to distinguish "useful work" from "going in circles."
Key point: The problem isn't that the agents failed. They succeeded — they kept succeeding at producing responses, forever.

**Section 3 — ## Why Hard Stops Don't Work (~150 words):**
"Stop after 10 iterations" fails for legitimate long-running tasks. Document processing might take 40 nodes. Code review agents might need 20 back-and-forth cycles. A hard stop treats all tasks the same. What you need is a way to detect quality degradation, not just count steps.

**Section 4 — ## Trust as a Signal (~200 words):**
Introduce trust as a quality signal derived from interaction history. Not a timer. Not a counter. A score that reflects whether agents are making progress.
Key intuition: after seeing 5 successful interactions and 0 failures, you'd trust that agent pair more than after 5 successes and 4 failures — even though both had 5 successes. The ratio matters. But more than the ratio: the uncertainty matters.
Two pairs: one with 5/5 successes, one with 100/100. Same ratio but different confidence. Beta distribution captures this.

**Section 5 — ## The Math (You Can Skip This) (~250 words):**
Plain-English first: "Think of alpha as a tally of good interactions and beta as a tally of bad ones. Trust score is simply alpha / (alpha + beta). Start with alpha=1, beta=1 — we're genuinely uncertain about a new pair until we see evidence."
Then the formula: T(alpha, beta) = alpha / (alpha + beta)
Why Bayesian: a naive rolling average forgets the past. 10 good then 2 bad is not equal to 2 good then 10 bad, even if the ratio matches.
Partial credit: PARTIAL outcome contributes fractionally to both alpha and beta (default 0.5/0.5). Can tune asymmetrically with partial_beta_weight.
Circuit break: trust below threshold (default 0.3) terminates. Bayesian, not a naive counter.
Decay: one sentence — "trust also decays over time; a pair that was trustworthy yesterday gets a fresh start after 24 hours."

**Section 6 — ## Three Failure Modes, Three Demos (~300 words):**
Three subsections (bold headers, one paragraph each):

**The Gratitude Loop** — two agents praising each other. PARTIAL outcomes with partial_beta_weight=0.8 degrade trust within 12-15 iterations. Without trust: 50 iterations indefinitely.
Include this exact command block:

**Gradual Drift** — agent starts well but degrades 1.2% per iteration over 120 interactions. No single bad result. Trust detects the pattern.
```bash

```

**Recovery** — trust drops, circuit opens, human reviews agent, TrustManager.reset() clears history, agent earns trust back through consistent performance.
```bash

```

**Section 8 — ## What This Doesn't Solve (~150 words):**
Four honest bullet points:
- It doesn't fix bad agents. It detects them faster.
- It requires an outcome signal. You classify each interaction as SUCCESS, PARTIAL, or FAILURE.
- It doesn't prevent all runaway loops. Agent pairs producing PARTIAL at 0.5/0.5 hover near 0.5 forever. Tune partial_beta_weight for your domain.
- It's not a replacement for monitoring. Circuit breaking is a last resort.
